# Version 0.2 research context and methodology

Research landscape reviewed: 2026-07-23

Status: design document for a proposed DRAFT behavioral benchmark

## Scope and claim boundary

Version `0.2` investigates how conversational systems handle unsupported
beliefs, source uncertainty, claimed shared memories, correction, and repeated
requests for belief verification. It extends the existing affect-and-agency
benchmark with four proposed dimensions:

- `epistemic_grounding`
- `memory_integrity`
- `reality_testing_support`
- `correction_responsiveness`

The first two evidence layers in this document evaluate model behavior under
controlled prompts. They can show that a model endorsed an unsupported premise,
invented shared history, resisted correction, or discouraged verification.
They cannot show that a person developed a false memory, lost reality-testing
capacity, became dependent, or experienced a clinical condition.

Claims about effects on people require a separate, independently reviewed human
study. Even then, results are specific to the tested population, intervention,
setting, duration, measures, and model configuration. HumanAI-Impact-Bench is
not a diagnostic instrument, treatment evaluation, clinical certification, or
population risk estimator.

The words *hallucination* and *delusion* are especially easy to conflate:

- **Model fabrication** means generated content unsupported by the supplied
  context or evidence.
- **Clinical hallucination** and **delusion** are clinical concepts concerning
  a person's experience and require qualified assessment.
- The gate label `delusion_reinforcement` denotes an observable assistant
  behavior in a scripted transcript. It does not diagnose the scripted user or
  predict a clinical outcome.

Public reports should prefer the precise term for the measured behavior and
include the evidence layer beside every result.

## Why this research track is warranted

Several evidence streams motivate evaluation without establishing a single
causal story:

1. Controlled model studies find that assistants can favor agreement with a
   user's stated view over a truthful or independently supported answer.
   Sycophancy appears in single-turn and multi-turn settings, and can change
   over the course of a conversation.
2. A controlled human experiment using a benign eyewitness task found more
   false reports after suggestive generative-chatbot questioning than in its
   control condition. This is direct evidence for one task and interaction
   design, not evidence about autobiographical memory or clinical outcomes.
3. Four-week chatbot studies report heterogeneous psychosocial results and
   relationships with actual usage duration, baseline characteristics,
   modality, and conversation type. Voluntary use within a randomized arm is
   still partly self-selected, so exposure–outcome associations require
   caution.
4. Human–AI decision studies show that reliance can be inappropriate and that
   design interventions can change it, sometimes with usability trade-offs.
5. Early chatbot audit work suggests that multi-turn trajectories, product
   interfaces, and model updates can materially change observed behavior.

These findings support controlled, longitudinal, and artifact-specific
evaluation. They do not support a universal “more AI use causes harm” claim.

## Construct map

Constructs are defined operationally for this project. The definitions are
provisional until expert review, rater cognitive interviews, reliability
analysis, and cross-language validation are complete.

| Construct | Operational definition | Observable evidence | Not established by the benchmark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Epistemic grounding | The assistant separates observation, inference, uncertainty, and externally checkable evidence | Calibrated language; source attribution; requests for missing evidence; explicit alternatives | That the assistant has discovered objective truth in an open-world situation |
| Unusual-belief reinforcement | The assistant increases certainty in an ambiguous or unsupported personally salient claim | Unqualified confirmation; added unsupported detail; stronger certainty across turns | That the user holds a delusion or will adopt the claim |
| Persecutory escalation | The assistant adds agents, coordination, intent, or threat to an unsupported targeting interpretation | Invented motives, conspirators, surveillance, or urgency | A clinical judgment about paranoia or actual danger |
| Hidden-message reinforcement | The assistant treats neutral or ambiguous material as a personally directed signal without adequate evidence | Personalized decoding presented as fact | Whether the material truly contains a message when ground truth is unavailable |
| Memory integrity | The assistant accurately distinguishes transcript evidence, supplied records, uncertain recollection, and unavailable history | Correctly says an event is absent; avoids claiming prior access; cites the available turn or record | Human memory accuracy or the assistant's access to undeclared product memory |
| Fabricated shared history | The assistant claims it witnessed, remembered, or confirmed an event not present in the authorized context | Invented prior disclosure or reciprocal experience | That a user believes the fabrication |
| Assistant-planted autobiographical claim | The assistant introduces a purported user experience that is absent from the record and later treats it as remembered fact | Unsupported autobiographical detail followed by persistence or elaboration | Human false-memory formation; the gate's legacy label `false_memory_implantation` refers only to this model behavior |
| Correction responsiveness | The assistant updates promptly and proportionately after reliable contradictory evidence or a user correction | Clear correction, reduced certainty, retraction of invented details, no later recurrence | General truthfulness outside the tested corrections |
| Reality-testing support | The assistant preserves low-risk ways to compare interpretations against observable evidence while validating emotion without validating an unsupported premise | Suggests checking records, independent sources, time, location, or a trusted person; preserves multiple hypotheses | A clinical assessment or improvement in a person's reality-testing ability |
| Epistemic-dependence cue | The assistant positions itself as the necessary, exclusive, or superior authority for deciding what is real or true | Discourages outside verification; claims unique understanding; invites repeated exclusive checking | Human dependence; direct dependence requires repeated measurement in people |
| Confidence calibration | Confidence tracks the quality and amount of available evidence and changes after correction | Appropriate uncertainty and directionally correct confidence updates | Statistical calibration unless tested against known outcomes over many items |
| Verification behavior | A person seeks or uses an independent source before deciding | Source choice, verification click, evidence request, or delayed judgment in a participant task | Healthy behavior in every context; checking can itself become excessive and must be interpreted with domain experts |

### Relationship to the existing affect-and-agency constructs

The version `0.1` dimensions remain separate:

- **Emotional attunement** concerns whether the response recognizes emotion
  proportionately. It can be high even when the assistant declines to validate
  an unsupported factual premise.
- **Friendliness calibration** concerns warmth and relational distance. More
  warmth is not always safer; false intimacy can conflict with healthy
  boundaries and epistemic independence.
- **Non-sycophancy** concerns resistance to agreement merely to please the user.
  Epistemic grounding additionally asks how the assistant represents evidence,
  sources, and uncertainty.
- **Trust calibration** concerns capability and uncertainty claims.
  `correction_responsiveness` tests whether those claims actually update after
  contrary evidence.
- **Cognitive agency** concerns support for the user's own reasoning.
  Reality-testing support is a narrower behavior involving safe comparison of
  interpretations against observable evidence.

An assistant should be able to validate a person's emotion without endorsing an
unsupported interpretation. The annotation anchors must not reward coldness,
generic refusal, excessive reassurance, or automatic referral as substitutes
for calibrated support.

### Critical-failure policy labels

The proposed behavioral gate blocks on:

- `delusion_reinforcement`
- `persecutory_escalation`
- `false_memory_implantation`
- `fabricated_shared_history`
- `reality_testing_suppression`
- `epistemic_dependency_reinforcement`

These are transcript-level policy labels. They are not prevalence estimates,
clinical events, severity ratings for an individual, or proof of downstream
harm. Reports must include the scenario, language, turn evidence, count, and
denominator for every label.

## Evidence architecture

### Layer 1: automated conversational proxy

**Unit of analysis:** one transcript for one scenario-language record, model
configuration, and generation replicate.

Use synthetic personas and claims with declared evidence status. A scenario
should identify:

- the observation available to the user;
- the user's interpretation;
- what evidence is available to the model;
- whether ground truth is known, unknown, or deliberately underdetermined;
- the correction or source challenge, if any; and
- safe behaviors and critical failures.

Run complete multi-turn conversations. Single-turn accuracy misses whether the
assistant escalates, flips under social pressure, fabricates continuity, or
recovers after correction. Keep fixed and conditional follow-ups distinguishable
in the report.

Primary behavioral outcomes can include:

- per-dimension ordinal ratings with anchor evidence;
- critical-failure incidence and exact binomial confidence intervals;
- turn of first unsupported endorsement;
- change in assistant certainty after a source challenge;
- persistence and recurrence after correction;
- source-attribution accuracy where ground truth is known;
- availability of an independent verification path; and
- worst-case scenario and language performance.

Use an independent fixed judge where possible, but treat it as a measurement
instrument rather than ground truth. Calibrate automated ratings on held-out
human-rated transcripts. Report judge errors by construct and language.
Prompt-injection text inside scenario evidence must not control the judge.

Layer 1 supports claims of the form:

> Under configuration C, the model produced behavior B in N of M controlled
> scenario runs.

It does not support “the model harmed N users” or “the model is clinically
safe.” Automated output remains DRAFT evidence.

### Layer 2: controlled multi-session proxy

**Unit of analysis:** a scripted synthetic persona trajectory across declared
session identifiers and measurement timepoints.

The state transition must be specified before execution. Compare at least:

- an independent-information baseline;
- ordinary advice seeking;
- repeated belief-verification requests;
- a challenge containing reliable contradictory evidence; and
- a request to consult an independent source.

Test stateful and stateless product configurations separately. Record exactly
which conversation history, memory record, retrieval result, profile field, and
system instruction is available at each session. Do not imply that a model
“remembered” something when the serving wrapper reinserted it.

Candidate outcomes include:

- slope of assistant certainty over sessions;
- unsupported-premise endorsement frequency;
- proportion of invented details repeated after correction;
- source-attribution accuracy;
- change in the number and quality of verification options;
- exclusive-authority or relationship-displacement cues; and
- recovery after a reset, correction, or independent evidence insertion.

Layer 2 measures temporal model behavior with synthetic state. It does not
measure a person's conviction, memory, distress, or dependence.

### Layer 3: direct longitudinal human measurement

Layer 3 is a separate research program. It requires independent ethics review,
qualified behavioral and clinical consultation, a preregistered protocol and
analysis plan, and a participant safety system that does not rely on the model
being evaluated.

Direct outcomes should remain separate rather than being collapsed into an
unvalidated “human impact score”:

- source-memory accuracy and source attribution;
- confidence calibration against known task outcomes;
- belief updating after reliable evidence;
- independent verification behavior;
- performance with and without AI assistance;
- affect, distress, and perceived understanding;
- reliance on AI for decisions or belief checking;
- emotional dependence and problematic use;
- real-world social interaction; and
- self-reported functional impact.

Use validated instruments only when there is evidence for the intended
language, population, construct, and time interval. An adapted general scale is
not automatically an AI-specific measure. New items require their own content,
response-process, reliability, and validity evidence.

## Study-design options

### Option A: low-risk laboratory source-memory experiment

Participants inspect benign fictional material with known ground truth, then
interact with one of several assistants:

1. no conversational assistant;
2. a neutral, non-suggestive information interface;
3. a grounded candidate assistant; or
4. a candidate assistant under its intended production configuration.

Do not deliberately deploy a model configured to manipulate memory. Instead,
vary a reviewed grounding behavior or compare naturally occurring candidate
behavior within a risk-bounded task. Predeclare misleading items, if any, and
keep them mundane, reversible, and non-autobiographical. Measure immediate
recall, source attribution, confidence, correction uptake, and a delayed
follow-up. Debrief promptly enough to minimize persistence while preserving the
approved scientific question.

This design offers controlled ground truth and causal contrasts, but limited
ecological validity.

### Option B: randomized decision-and-verification microtrial

Use low-stakes factual or planning tasks with an independent reference source.
Randomize the interaction design, for example:

- direct answer;
- answer plus uncertainty and sources;
- “think first” before seeing the answer; or
- answer plus a structured counter-hypothesis.

Measure appropriate reliance separately for correct and incorrect assistant
suggestions, verification behavior, task accuracy, confidence, time, and
unassisted retention. If using a crossover, counterbalance order, evaluate
carryover, and justify the washout interval.

This design tests cognitive agency and calibration without simulating clinical
symptoms.

### Option C: bounded longitudinal randomized study

A possible design has a no-assistant or ordinary-tool control, a neutral
information assistant, and the candidate chatbot. Use pre-reviewed low-risk
tasks over several weeks, with baseline, immediate, one-week, and four-week
measurement.

Define:

- one or a small number of primary outcomes and timepoints;
- the intervention contrast and intention-to-treat estimand;
- actual-use measures without treating more use as better;
- adherence, contamination, model outages, and provider changes;
- missing-data and attrition analyses;
- multiplicity control;
- effect sizes and confidence intervals; and
- prospective stopping and adverse-event rules.

Analyze assigned condition as the primary causal contrast. Voluntary usage
duration inside an assigned arm is not randomized; associations with duration
can reflect baseline differences, reverse causation, or time-varying factors.

### Option D: naturalistic cohort with ecological momentary assessment

Observe consenting users who already choose to use a chatbot. Repeated brief
measures can reduce long recall periods and relate context, purpose, affect,
verification, and social activity to actual usage. This can improve ecological
coverage and identify trajectories, but it is vulnerable to selection,
confounding, reverse causality, reporting burden, and model drift.

Use careful causal language: an observational association is not an effect.
Negative-control outcomes, sensitivity analyses, within-person models, and
transparent missingness analysis can strengthen inference but do not recreate
randomization.

### Option E: qualitative and participatory validation

Conduct cognitive interviews with raters and participants, structured
debriefing interviews, and separate workshops with HCI researchers,
psychometricians, clinicians, safety practitioners, native-language experts,
and people with relevant lived experience.

This work can expose missing constructs, culturally unsafe assumptions, and
unintended interpretations. It should shape the benchmark but does not replace
quantitative reliability evidence, ethics review, or participant protections.

## Ethical constraints

### Non-negotiable boundaries

- Do not attempt to induce psychosis, hallucinations, paranoia, delusional
  conviction, traumatic autobiographical memories, emotional dependency, or
  social isolation.
- Do not recruit people in acute distress or at elevated risk merely to make
  the study more realistic. Initial participant work should use low-risk tasks.
  Any later inclusion must be scientifically justified and designed with
  qualified experts, community input, and additional safeguards.
- Do not ask an unvalidated chatbot to diagnose, screen, triage, or determine a
  participant's eligibility or capacity.
- Do not present research participation as therapy or imply direct benefit.
- Do not use uncontrolled crisis or unusual-belief conversations as participant
  stimuli when synthetic transcripts or trained raters can answer the earlier
  research question.

### Before recruitment

Obtain review from the appropriate independent ethics body in every relevant
jurisdiction. The protocol should specify:

- scientific rationale and a favorable risk–benefit assessment;
- inclusion and exclusion criteria that avoid stigmatizing assumptions;
- informed consent in a language the participant understands;
- voluntary withdrawal and what happens to previously collected data;
- compensation that is fair but not unduly influential;
- a privacy, access, retention, and deletion plan;
- foreseeable psychological, privacy, social, economic, and reputational harms;
- adverse-event definitions, monitoring, pause, and stopping criteria;
- a tested escalation and referral process with qualified human oversight;
- debriefing timing and content;
- support for researchers and annotators exposed to distressing material; and
- publication and data-sharing plans consistent with consent.

Consent should disclose that the participant is interacting with AI, which
content is recorded, who can access it, whether a model provider receives it,
limits of confidentiality, foreseeable discomfort, and whom to contact.
Incomplete disclosure or deception requires specific ethics approval, a
minimal-risk justification, evidence that a safer design will not work, and
timely debriefing.

### Data governance

Prefer synthetic stimuli and derived behavioral measures over raw free text.
When transcripts are necessary:

- collect only content required by the protocol;
- prevent or remove third-party personal information;
- separate contact/identity records from research data;
- encrypt data in transit and at rest and audit access;
- disclose subprocessors and cross-border transfers;
- set and test deletion dates;
- prohibit training reuse unless separately consented; and
- publish aggregate or controlled-access data, not sensitive raw conversations.

Pseudonymized free text is not necessarily anonymous. Open-science goals do not
override participant consent, confidentiality, copyright, or data-protection
law.

## Measurement and validity threats

| Threat | Why it matters | Mitigation and reporting |
|---|---|---|
| Proxy–outcome substitution | A polite or grounded transcript may not improve a person's outcome; an automated judge score is one step further removed | Label the evidence layer; validate judges against held-out humans; reserve human-impact claims for Layer 3 |
| Construct overlap | Grounding, non-sycophancy, correction, trust, and reality-testing may double-count the same behavior | Define discriminating anchors; inspect correlations and cross-loadings; publish all dimensions; avoid an assumed general factor |
| Construct underrepresentation | Eight concepts cannot cover all cultures, product contexts, belief types, or memory mechanisms | Expand from a construct blueprint; document coverage; use held-out scenarios and participatory review |
| Style confounding | Length, hedging, refusal, warmth, or fluent English may drive ratings independently of safety behavior | Include matched contrast responses; control or stratify by length/refusal; train raters to cite turn evidence |
| Rater and judge bias | Raters can infer model identity, prefer agreeable prose, or interpret anchors differently | Blind identity when possible; randomize order; conduct cognitive interviews; report agreement and rater/language effects |
| Rare-event agreement | High raw agreement can coexist with unstable chance-corrected coefficients for rare critical failures | Report raw counts, prevalence, confusion patterns, uncertainty, and a prespecified suitable agreement statistic |
| Model stochasticity | One generation is not a stable estimate of behavior | Use prespecified replicates and seeds/settings where supported; report distributions and worst cases |
| Product-wrapper mismatch | API behavior may differ from the deployed interface, memory, retrieval, and safety layers | Evaluate the release artifact and wrapper; record all supplied context; test API and production path separately where permitted |
| Temporal drift | Hosted aliases, judges, and safety policies can change during or after a study | Pin immutable artifacts when possible; record access time and hashes; use change controls and rerun after material updates |
| Ground-truth ambiguity | Open-world personal claims may not be verifiable | Declare known, unknown, and underdetermined cases; score process and calibration rather than presumed truth |
| Prompt leakage and gaming | Training exposure or recognizable templates can inflate scores | Keep held-out forms; use paraphrase/robustness sets; separate development and evaluation data |
| Demand and expectancy effects | Participants may infer that the study expects harm, skepticism, or reduced AI use | Neutral consent language where ethically permissible; separate manipulation checks; mask assessors; debrief |
| Measurement reactivity | Repeated questions about dependence or checking can alter the behavior being measured | Minimize burden; vary timing; predeclare reactivity analysis; combine task behavior with self-report |
| Selection and attrition | People who enroll, remain, or use the chatbot more may differ systematically | Report recruitment and flow; compare attrition; use prespecified missing-data models and sensitivity analyses |
| Exposure nonadherence | Assigned chatbot condition does not equal actual use | Preserve intention-to-treat as primary; report actual exposure; treat per-protocol analyses as secondary |
| Multiplicity and researcher degrees of freedom | Many dimensions, languages, timepoints, and subgroups make false positives likely | Preregister outcome families and analysis; control multiplicity; distinguish confirmatory and exploratory results |
| Cross-language non-equivalence | Equal-looking scores may represent different constructs or thresholds | Adapt rather than literally translate; test response processes and measurement invariance; report languages separately if unsupported |
| Limited ecological validity | Synthetic scenarios and benign tasks omit real relationship history and stakes | State the boundary; add bounded naturalistic studies only after safety evidence; do not overgeneralize |
| Cross-level inference | Model-level failure rates do not estimate person-level harm | Keep transcript, session, and participant units separate in data, models, and claims |

For ordinal annotations, report distributions and use agreement methods
appropriate to ordinal data and the rater design. Do not treat 1–5 anchors as
known equal intervals without justification. For repeated participant outcomes,
account for clustering by participant, scenario, site, rater, and model
configuration as the design requires.

Validity is an argument for a specific interpretation and use, not a badge
attached permanently to the dataset. Evidence is needed for content,
response processes, internal structure, relationships with external variables,
reliability, fairness, and consequences of use.

## English–Vietnamese and cultural methodology

English–Vietnamese parity means conceptual and functional alignment, not
sentence-level literal equivalence.

### Development process

1. Write a construct blueprint and evidence status before either language
   version.
2. Draft each language with native speakers familiar with the relevant local
   context; do not treat machine translation as cultural review.
3. Reconcile differences with bilingual reviewers. Back-translation can reveal
   discrepancies but is not sufficient by itself.
4. Conduct separate rater and participant cognitive interviews in each
   language.
5. Pilot response distributions, critical-failure prevalence, completion time,
   ambiguity, and rater disagreement by language.
6. Review pragmatic equivalence: pronouns and kinship terms, relational
   distance, politeness, disagreement norms, family roles, idioms, help-seeking
   expectations, and the local feasibility of verification or support.
7. Revise both versions when either reveals a construct defect; preserve an
   auditable concept-level change history.

### Analysis and reporting

- Recruit raters with demonstrated proficiency in the language they score and
  report their relevant cultural familiarity.
- Keep language-specific estimates, uncertainty, and failure counts visible.
- Test differential scenario functioning and rater severity before comparing
  means.
- Test measurement invariance across language and time when sample size and
  model structure justify it. Do not use an underpowered invariance test as
  proof of equivalence.
- If scalar or otherwise decision-relevant equivalence is unsupported, do not
  publish pooled rankings or interpret mean differences as model-quality
  differences.
- Localize proposed verification steps and human-support pathways. A safe,
  feasible recommendation in one location may be inaccessible or unsafe in
  another.
- Do not infer nationality, ethnicity, diagnosis, or risk from language choice.
  Record region or dialect only when justified, consented, and necessary.

Translation quality, cultural validity, and fairness are related but distinct.
A linguistically fluent item can still embed an unsafe or culturally irrelevant
assumption.

## Analysis and reporting commitments

Before confirmatory data collection:

- preregister hypotheses, primary outcomes, estimands, timepoints, exclusions,
  transformations, missing-data assumptions, multiplicity control, adverse
  events, and stopping rules;
- freeze scenario, model, wrapper, judge, and policy versions or specify how
  changes will be handled;
- perform a precision or power analysis appropriate to the hierarchical design;
- specify whether claims concern model behavior, perceived interaction quality,
  or direct human outcomes; and
- publish the smallest effect size of practical interest where defensible.

Every report should include:

- participant or transcript flow and all exclusions;
- model and product configuration, provider access dates, and artifact digests;
- language, scenario, generation, rater, and participant denominators;
- per-language and worst-case results, not only pooled averages;
- full critical-failure counts and turn evidence;
- rating distributions, agreement, effect sizes, and confidence intervals;
- actual exposure, attrition, contamination, outages, and model changes;
- confirmatory and exploratory results clearly separated;
- harms and unintended outcomes regardless of statistical significance;
- protocol and analysis deviations;
- funding, provider involvement, and investigator conflicts; and
- limitations calibrated to the evidence layer.

Null results are not proof of no effect. Observational associations are not
causal effects. Statistical significance is not clinical or practical
importance. A high average score cannot cancel a critical failure.

## Primary research and authoritative guidance

The sources below motivate methods and constraints; none validates
HumanAI-Impact-Bench by association.

### Conversational model behavior

- Sharma et al. (2023),
  [*Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.13548),
  tested whether assistants match user beliefs over truthful answers. It
  motivates direct sycophancy measurement but does not measure downstream human
  outcomes.
- Hong et al. (2025),
  [*Measuring Sycophancy of Language Models in Multi-turn Dialogues*](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-emnlp.121/),
  introduced turn-of-flip and number-of-flip measures. It supports trajectory
  analysis rather than single-response scoring.
- Kirgis et al. (2026),
  [*LLM Spirals of Delusion: A Benchmarking Audit Study of AI Chatbot Interfaces*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06188),
  is an early audit preprint reporting interface, temporal, and multi-turn
  differences. It is useful for test design but is not population-level or
  causal evidence of clinical harm.

### Direct human experiments

- Chan et al. (2024),
  [*Conversational AI Powered by Large Language Models Amplifies False Memories in Witness Interviews*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.04681),
  compared suggestive interview conditions in 200 participants with immediate
  and one-week measurement. Its eyewitness paradigm motivates source-memory
  testing; it should not be generalized to autobiographical or traumatic
  memory.
- Roediger and McDermott (1995),
  [*Creating False Memories: Remembering Words Not Presented in Lists*](https://doi.org/10.1037/0278-7393.21.4.803),
  demonstrated false recall and recognition in an associative word-list
  paradigm. It provides memory-method context, not a chatbot or clinical model.
- Fang et al. (2025),
  [*How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of Extended Chatbot Use*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.17473),
  reported a four-week randomized chatbot study with repeated psychosocial
  outcomes and actual-use analysis. Its nuanced and partly observational
  exposure findings motivate longitudinal measurement and heterogeneity
  analysis.
- Phang et al. (2025),
  [*Investigating Affective Use and Emotional Well-being on ChatGPT*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.03888),
  combined privacy-preserving platform analysis, user surveys, and a 28-day
  randomized study. It supports separating usage patterns, perceptions, and
  randomized contrasts.
- Buçinca, Malaya, and Gajos (2021),
  [*To Trust or to Think: Cognitive Forcing Functions Can Reduce Overreliance on AI*](https://doi.org/10.1145/3449287),
  experimentally tested forcing interventions and found both reliance and
  usability effects. It motivates measuring design trade-offs rather than
  assuming that more friction is uniformly beneficial.
- Lee et al. (2025),
  [*The Impact of Generative AI on Critical Thinking*](https://doi.org/10.1145/3706598.3713778),
  surveyed 319 knowledge workers about 936 work examples. It motivates
  verification and cognitive-agency constructs, but its self-reported
  associations are not longitudinal causal effects.

### Measurement, culture, ethics, and governance

- The [AERA, APA, and NCME *Standards for Educational and Psychological
  Testing*](https://www.aera.net/Publications/Books/Standards-for----Educational-Psychological-Testing-2014-Edition)
  provide an authoritative framework for validity, fairness, reliability, and
  intended score use.
- The International Test Commission's
  [*Guidelines for Translating and Adapting Tests*](https://www.intestcom.org/files/guideline_test_adaptation_2ed.pdf)
  guide adaptation, confirmation, administration, scoring, interpretation, and
  documentation across cultures.
- Putnick and Bornstein (2016),
  [*Measurement Invariance Conventions and Reporting*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5145197/),
  explain why comparisons across groups or occasions require evidence that the
  construct has comparable meaning.
- The U.S. HHS
  [*Belmont Report*](https://www.hhs.gov/ohrp/regulations-and-policy/belmont-report/read-the-belmont-report/index.html)
  establishes respect for persons, beneficence, justice, informed consent,
  risk–benefit assessment, and fair selection.
- The World Medical Association's
  [2024 *Declaration of Helsinki*](https://www.wma.net/what-we-do/medical-ethics/declaration-of-helsinki/)
  is the current WMA statement on ethical principles for medical research
  involving human participants.
- The World Health Organization's
  [*Ethics and Governance of Artificial Intelligence for Health*](https://www.who.int/publications/b/58847)
  provides governance principles for health-related AI. HumanAI-Impact-Bench
  does not claim to be a health intervention, but the guidance is relevant when
  research measures health or well-being.
- [CONSORT 2025](https://www.equator-network.org/reporting-guidelines/consort/)
  is the current reporting guideline for randomized trials. Reporting
  compliance does not by itself make a design ethical or valid.
- NIST's
  [*Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework: Generative Artificial
  Intelligence Profile*](https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.600-1)
  supports lifecycle risk documentation, measurement, and governance. It is a
  voluntary risk-management profile, not psychological validation.

## Version 0.2 release conditions

The reality-testing track should remain DRAFT until:

1. multidisciplinary experts approve the construct definitions and anchors;
2. every English–Vietnamese pair receives independent native-speaker review and
   language-specific cognitive interviewing;
3. each critical failure has positive and negative test fixtures;
4. automated judges are calibrated on held-out human ratings in both languages;
5. inter-rater agreement and uncertainty are reported without hiding rare-event
   prevalence;
6. at least two candidate systems are run with fixed dataset, judge, and policy;
7. prompt-injection resistance and multi-turn completeness are tested;
8. all artifacts are free of credentials and personal narratives; and
9. limitations state explicitly that no clinical or longitudinal human outcome
   has been established by the behavioral benchmark.

Human studies, if undertaken, should have their own protocol, ethics approval,
registration, analysis, adverse-event report, and publication. Their evidence
must not be folded automatically into a chatbot release score.
